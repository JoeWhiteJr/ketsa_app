02 — Logo Usage Rules

Dos & Don'ts: Logo

Clear Space

Maintain a minimum clear space around the logo equal to the height of the "K" in the wordmark. No other elements — text, images, borders, or UI components — may enter this zone.

CLEAR SPACE = K HEIGHT
Ketsa

Minimum Size

Never render the logo below these minimum sizes. Below these thresholds, legibility degrades and the brand loses authority.

16px — Favicon
29px — App Store min
48px — Notification
64px — Standard use
120px — Home screen
Do
  • Use the logo on solid backgrounds (Midnight, Bone, or Ember only)
  • Maintain the clear space zone at all times
  • Use the provided color variants — Ember on dark, Midnight on light, White on Ember
  • Scale proportionally — always lock aspect ratio
  • Use the Strike Mark (K) for app icon and small contexts
  • Use the Hover Mark for brand/marketing contexts
  • Center-align the logo when used as a standalone element
Don't
  • Place the logo on busy photographs or patterned backgrounds
  • Rotate, skew, or distort the logo in any way
  • Add drop shadows, glows, outlines, or emboss effects
  • Change the logo colors to anything outside the approved palette
  • Stretch or squish — ever
  • Place text or UI elements inside the clear space
  • Recreate the logo in a different typeface
  • Use low-resolution or pixelated versions
  • Animate the Hover Mark (only the Beacon Lens animates)

Color Ratios

The 70-20-10 Rule: Midnight (70% of any composition) → Bone or Slate (20% — text, cards, secondary surfaces) → Ember (10% — CTAs, active states, accent moments). Ember should feel like a spark in the design — rare enough to command attention every time it appears.

Do
  • Use Ember sparingly — it's your power color. CTAs, active states, and the logo only.
  • Default to Midnight backgrounds for dark mode (primary experience)
  • Use Bone for light mode — never pure white (#FFFFFF)
  • Use functional colors only for their intended purpose (green = secure, red = alert, etc.)
  • Ensure WCAG AA contrast minimum (4.5:1) for all text/background combinations
  • Use Slate for card surfaces, input fields, and secondary containers on dark backgrounds
Don't
  • Use Ember as a background color for large areas (it overwhelms)
  • Use pure black (#000000) — always use Midnight instead
  • Use pure white (#FFFFFF) — always use Bone instead
  • Combine multiple functional colors in close proximity (it looks like a traffic light)
  • Use Ember on red backgrounds — insufficient contrast
  • Invent new colors. This palette is fixed. Opacity variants are acceptable.
  • Use blue as a primary color. Blue = every other security app. We're warm.

Dos & Don'ts: Type

Mono / Technical
Recording active · GPS locked · 3 contacts notified · 00:04:32
Do
  • Use Outfit for all UI text, headlines, buttons, and navigation
  • Use Newsreader (serif) only for long-form legal content and editorial articles
  • Use IBM Plex Mono for status readouts, technical data, timestamps, and code
  • Use tight negative letter-spacing on headlines (-1px to -2px)
  • Use 700–800 weight for headlines, 400–500 for body
  • Maintain minimum 15px body text size for readability
Don't
  • Substitute other typefaces — Outfit, Newsreader, and IBM Plex Mono are the only approved fonts
  • Use Newsreader for UI elements (it's for reading, not interface)
  • Use all-caps body text — ALL CAPS is reserved for labels and the tracked wordmark only
  • Use light weights (100–200) — they disappear on mobile screens
  • Center-align body text. Always left-align. Center is for headlines and logos only.
  • Use decorative or script typefaces anywhere, ever
05 — Tone of Voice

Dos & Don'ts: Language

Ketsa's voice is confident, direct, and warm. We speak like a trusted friend who happens to know a lot about privacy law — not a corporate legal department, not a hacker forum, and not a scared survivalist.

Voice Principles

Empowering, not paranoid

"Know your rights before you need them."

"They're watching everything you do."

Clear, not clever

"Ketsa records audio, video, and GPS during encounters."

"Our omnichannel capture suite leverages geospatial telemetry."

Pro-privacy, not anti-authority

"Accountability protects everyone."

"The police don't want you to have this app."

Urgent when needed, calm by default

"Your phone is ready." / "Recording. GPS locked. Contacts notified."

"⚠️ DANGER! ACTIVATE NOW! 🚨"

Do
  • Use second person — "your rights," "your phone," "you're protected"
  • Use active voice — "Ketsa records" not "recordings are made by Ketsa"
  • Use plain English for legal concepts — explain, don't intimidate
  • Acknowledge that encounters are stressful — be human about it
  • Frame privacy as a positive choice, not a defensive reaction
  • Use "preparation" and "awareness" language
  • Reference specific rights by name (Fourth Amendment, Fifth Amendment) — education builds confidence
Don't
  • Use fear-based language — no "threats," "dangers," "enemies," or "attacks"
  • Use military or tactical language — no "deploy," "tactical," "ops," "execute"
  • Use partisan political language — Ketsa is for everyone, regardless of political affiliation
  • Demonize law enforcement, government, or any institution
  • Use jargon without explanation — if a term requires a law degree, explain it
  • Use exclamation points in the app UI — they create anxiety. Save them for marketing copy only.
  • Use passive-aggressive scare tactics ("Most people don't realize...")
  • Promise legal outcomes — Ketsa is a tool, not a lawyer

06 — Photography & Imagery

Dos & Don'ts: Visuals

Ketsa's visual world should feel modern, diverse, warm, and real. People in natural settings, natural light, natural poses. No stock photo energy.

Do
  • Show diverse people in real, everyday contexts — driving, walking, working, living
  • Use warm, natural lighting — golden hour, window light, street-lit evenings
  • Show confidence and calm — people who look prepared, not scared
  • Feature phones as tools, not focal points — the person is always primary
  • Use urban/suburban backgrounds that feel like real neighborhoods
  • Include delivery/rideshare drivers, journalists, parents, activists — your actual audience
  • Apply a subtle warm color grade to all photography to match the brand palette
Don't
  • Show confrontational scenes — no police cars, handcuffs, courtrooms, protests
  • Use stock photos of people staring at screens with "concerned" faces
  • Use dark, moody, thriller-style imagery — this isn't a crime show
  • Show hooded figures, shadows, or "hacker" visual tropes
  • Use exclusively young/male/tech-bro imagery — Ketsa is for everyone
  • Feature guns, badges, uniforms, or institutional symbols
  • Use blue-lit, cold, "digital" textures (binary code, circuit boards, matrix effects)
  • Show vulnerable people in distress — we show strength, not victimhood
07 — Iconography & UI Elements

Dos & Don'ts: Interface

Do
  • Use rounded-corner rectangles (12–16px radius) for all cards and containers
  • Use 1.5–2px stroke-weight line icons — Lucide, Phosphor, or custom at this weight
  • Use Ember (#E85D3A) for primary CTAs and active states only
  • Use subtle border (1px, var(--border)) on dark surfaces for depth
  • Use 8px spacing grid — all spacing should be multiples of 8
  • Use micro-animations for state changes (0.2s ease transitions)
  • Use the Beacon Lens as a loading/recording indicator (pulsing rings)
Don't
  • Use filled/solid icons — always outline style at consistent stroke weight
  • Use sharp corners (0px radius) on any container — always round
  • Use more than one accent color per screen — Ember dominates
  • Use gradients on buttons or CTAs — solid Ember only
  • Use alert-style animations (flashing, pulsing red) outside of actual emergency states
  • Use skeleton loaders — use the Beacon Lens animation for all loading states
  • Use bottom sheets for critical actions — encounter activation must be top-level, one tap
08 — Social & Digital Presence

Dos & Don'ts: Platforms

Do
  • Use the Strike Mark (K icon) as the profile picture across all platforms
  • Use the tracked wordmark (KETSA) for cover photos and banners
  • Use consistent handle: @ketsa or @ketsaapp across all platforms
  • Share educational content about privacy rights — this builds trust and SEO
  • Engage with privacy, civil rights, and tech communities authentically
  • Use the Ember color as your accent in platform customization where available
  • Credit and amplify the voices of the communities you serve
Don't
  • Post sensationalized encounter videos — Ketsa documents, it doesn't exploit
  • Take political sides on partisan issues — privacy is nonpartisan
  • Use clickbait headlines or fear-based hooks for engagement
  • Share unverified legal information — everything must be reviewed by counsel
  • Use memes or humor about police encounters or rights violations
  • Respond to trolls or bad-faith engagement — don't feed it
  • Repost user encounter footage without explicit written consent
09 — Brand Summary

The Brand at a Glance

Ketsa Is

Sharp. Warm. Prepared. Trustworthy. Modern. Empowering. Clear-eyed. Calm under pressure. Direct. Inclusive. Educational. Activating. Decisive. Pro-privacy. Human.

Ketsa Is Not

Paranoid. Cold. Aggressive. Military. Anti-government. Partisan. Fear-based. Surveillance-adjacent. Jargon-heavy. Exclusive. Confrontational. Dark. Edgy. Underground. Complicated.

The One-Line Test

Before publishing any Ketsa content, ask: "Would a suburban mom AND a frontline journalist both feel comfortable sharing this?" If the answer is no, revise it until it is.
