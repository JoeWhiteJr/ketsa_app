01 — The Name

Why Ketsa Works

Ketsa is a coined word with no established meaning in any major language. It evokes the kestrel — the hovering falcon known for holding perfectly still in midair while watching the ground below — without being a direct reference. It carries echoes of sharp, kinetic words across multiple languages: ketsu (Japanese: determination), ketsav (Hebrew: hawk-like), kestrel (English: the smallest falcon).

The name is five letters, two syllables, and begins with a hard K — one of the most memorable consonant openings in branding (Kodak, Kayak, Kindle, Klarna). The -tsa ending is unusual in English, which creates distinctiveness without sacrificing pronounceability.

05 — Visual Identity

Logo Concept Directions

Three distinct approaches, each designed to work at every scale — from 29×29px app icon to billboard. The visual identity should feel sharp, warm, and trustworthy — a counterpoint to the cold blues and greens that dominate the privacy app market.

01

The Hover

Inspired by the kestrel falcon, frozen in midair

A single geometric bird shape — not realistic, abstracted to pure angles — captured in the moment of hovering. Wings slightly angled, body still, head tilted down watching. The silhouette should read as a stylized K at certain angles (an easter egg, not forced). Rendered in the accent color (ember orange) against dark backgrounds, or reversed to dark on light.

Why it works: The kestrel's hover is the perfect metaphor — perfectly still, intensely focused, watching everything below. It's a bird of prey, but the posture is patient, not attacking. It communicates vigilance without aggression. At small sizes, the angular wings create an instantly recognizable mark.

Execution: Maximum 8–10 points in the vector path. This must be geometric enough to feel like a tech logo, organic enough to feel alive. Test at 16px favicon size — if it's still recognizable, you've nailed it.

02

The Strike Mark

Kinetic energy captured in a single gesture

A bold, diagonal slash — like a talon mark or a quick pen stroke — that also reads as the letter K. One continuous motion, thick to thin, suggesting speed and decisiveness. This isn't a bird; it's the feeling of a bird's dive. The mark sits at an angle (roughly 15° off vertical) to create dynamic energy even when static.

Why it works: When you activate Ketsa during an encounter, you need the app to feel like it's doing something immediately. This mark communicates instant action. It's also the most versatile — it works as a standalone icon, an animated loading state (the stroke draws itself), and a watermark on recorded video.

Execution: The stroke should have visible pressure dynamics — thicker at the point of impact, tapering to a sharp end. Think calligraphy, not illustration. One color only. The angle is critical — too steep feels like a slash (aggressive), too shallow feels like an underline (passive). 15° is the sweet spot.

03

The Beacon Lens

An eye that protects, not surveils

A concentric circle motif — like a camera lens, a ripple from a GPS pin, or a focused eye — with a warm center point that radiates outward. Two to three rings, tight spacing, with the innermost circle solid (the "iris"). The rings can subtly animate in the app (pulsing when active, static when idle).

Why it works: It ties directly to all three pillars: the lens = recording, the rings = GPS signal broadcast, the eye = awareness/education. The warm color palette prevents it from feeling like surveillance tech. At app-icon size, the concentric circles are instantly distinctive on a home screen.

Execution: The rings should have slightly uneven spacing (tighter toward the center) to create organic tension. The center dot should be slightly off-center — not a bullseye, more like an iris. This prevents the "target" association. Warm gradient on the center point (ember to gold) against midnight background.

Logo Recommendation

Lead with Concept 01 (The Hover) as your primary brand mark and Concept 02 (The Strike Mark) as your app icon. The Hover carries the brand story — it's what goes on the website, pitch deck, and merch. The Strike Mark is what lives on the phone — it's faster, simpler, and more impactful at small sizes. Having a distinct brand mark and app icon is standard practice for premium app brands (Notion, Slack, Discord all do this).

07 — Typography

Type System

Ketsa
Wordmark — Outfit ExtraBold, tracked tight. Sharp geometric sans with personality.
KETSA
Alternative wordmark — Outfit ExtraBold, tracked wide, all caps. Campaign use, app splash screen.

Type Pairing

  • Headlines & UI: Outfit (600–800 weight) — geometric, modern, distinctive without being trendy. The round O and sharp K give it character.
  • Body & Editorial: Newsreader — a transitional serif for long-form legal guides and educational content. Adds warmth and readability to the legal education pillar.
  • Mono / Technical: IBM Plex Mono — for settings screens, privacy audit readouts, status indicators, and legal citations. Clinical precision where needed.

Rationale: The Outfit + Newsreader pairing gives you two voices — one sharp and modern (the app itself), one warm and authoritative (the legal content). This dual-register approach serves both the "Ketsa up before your shift" user and the "I'm reading about Fourth Amendment case law" user without creating cognitive dissonance.
